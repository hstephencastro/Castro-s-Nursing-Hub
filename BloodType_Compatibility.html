0️⃣
O = Zero
Think of "O" as the number ZERO. Zero antigens on the cell surface = zero reasons to reject it. That's why O− is the universal donor — no antigens to trigger a reaction in any recipient. BUT: O blood has antibodies against A AND B, so it can only accept O blood. It gives freely but is very picky about what it receives.
➕
Positive = Permissive, Negative = Picky
Rh POSITIVE patients are PERMISSIVE — they accept either positive OR negative blood, no questions asked. Rh NEGATIVE patients are PICKY — they REFUSE positive blood. If an Rh− patient receives Rh+ blood, the body builds antibodies (sensitization). This is especially critical for pregnant women!
👑
AB = Accepts Both
AB literally ACCEPTS BOTH A and B antigens on its own cells — so it never makes antibodies against either. That's what makes AB the universal recipient. The catch: AB can only DONATE to other AB people (too many antigens for anyone else to tolerate).
📺
The House / Wilson Rule
From the TV show House MD: Wilson tells House "You're AB — universal recipient, you TAKE from everybody." House replies: "You're O — universal donor. No wonder you're always giving." House takes, Wilson gives. Remember the characters, remember the types!
🔤
Same Letter + O Rule
Recipients can always receive their own type PLUS type O. Type A → accepts A or O. Type B → accepts B or O. Type AB → accepts AB, A, B, or O (everyone!). Type O → accepts ONLY O. Quick check: does the donor letter match the recipient's letter, or is it O?
🤰
Rh− Women → RhoGAM
An Rh− mother carrying an Rh+ baby MUST receive RhoGAM to prevent sensitization. If her blood mixes with the baby's (at delivery or miscarriage), she forms anti-Rh antibodies — and those antibodies will attack future Rh+ pregnancies. RhoGAM neutralizes the threat before antibodies form.
